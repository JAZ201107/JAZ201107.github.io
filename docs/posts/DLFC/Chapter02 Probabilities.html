In most every application of machine learning we have to deal with uncertainty, there are two type of uncertainty:

  • Epistemic uncertainty(also called systematic uncertainty):
    • Arise because we only get to see data sets of finite size
    • Solution: Gather more data
  • Aleatoric uncertainty(also called intrinsic uncertainty, stochastic uncertainty, or noise )
    • Arise because we observe only partial information about the world
    • Solution: Gather different kinds of data.

Both uncertainty can be handled using the framework of probability theory. When couple with decision theory, allow us to make optimal predictions given all the information available to us.

:::{.callout-important} Sum Rule & Product Rule

Two most important formulae are:

\[ \begin{gather} p(X) = \begin{cases} \sum_{Y}p(X ,Y) \\ \\ \int p(X,Y) \, dy \end{cases} &\text{(sum rule)}\\ p(X, Y) = p(Y|X)p(X) &\text{(product rule)} \end{gather} \]

2.1 The Rules of Probability

\(X\) represent the presence of absence of cancer, \(Y\) represent the outcome of the test. \(X\) and \(Y\) are called random variables or stochastic variables. \(X\) can take \(x_{i}\) where \(i = 1, \dots, L\) and \(Y\) can take \(j\), where \(j=1, \dots, M\).

  • \(n_{ij}\): the number of trial that \(X=x_{i}\) and \(Y=y_{j}\)
  • \(c_{i}\): the number of trial that \(X=x_{i}\), irrespective of the value of \(Y\)
  • \(r_{j}\): the number of trial that \(Y=y_{j}\), irrespective of the value of \(X\)

So, the sum rule(sometimes called marginal probability) is:

\[ p(X=x_{i}) = \sum_{j=1}^{M}p(X=x_{i}, Y=y_{j}) \]

The conditional probability is \[p(Y=y_{j} | X=x_{i})\] The joint probability is:

\[ p(Y=y_{j}, X=x+i) \]

And the product rule is:

\[ p(X=x_{i}, Y=y_{j}) = p(Y=y_{j} | X= x_{i}) p(X=x_{i}) \]

2.1.3 Bayes’ theorem

Bayes’ theorem is the relationship between conditional probabilities:

\[p(Y|X) = \frac{p(X|Y)p(Y)}{p(X)}\]

The denominator is the Bayes’ theorem as the normalisation constant.

2.1.4 Medical Screening Example

\(C\) denotes the presence(1) or absence(0) of cancer. We have prior probability:

\[ \begin{gather} p(C = 1) = 1 / 100 \\ p(C= 0) = 99 / 100 \end{gather} \]

\(T\) represent the outcome of a screening test. \(T=1\) represent test positive, while \(T=0\) represent test negative. And we known that

\[ \begin{align} p(T =1 |C=1) = 90 / 100 \\ p(T =0 |C=1) = 10 / 100 \\ p(T =1 |C=0) = 3 / 100 \\ p(T =0 |C=0) = 97 / 100 \\ \end{align} \]

So, we can use sum rule and product rule to calculate the probability that someone test randomly will get \(T=1\):

\[ p(T=1) = \sum_{c}p(T=1|C=c)p(C=c) = 0.0387 \]

If \(T=1\), then what is \(p(C=1|T=1)\)

\[ p(C=1|T=1) = \frac{p(T=1|C=1)p(C=1)}{p(T=1)} \approx 0.23 \]

2.1.6 Independent variables

If the joint distribution of two variables factorised into the product of the marginals:

\[ p(X, Y) = p(X)p(Y) \]

then, \(X\) and \(Y\) are called independent. And two independent variables has following property from the product rule

\[ \begin{gather} p(X, Y) = \\ p(Y|X)p(X) =p(X)p(Y) \\ \implies p(Y|X) = p(Y) \end{gather} \]

2.2 Probability Densities

Probability density \(p(x)\) over a continuous variable \(x\) to be such that the probability of \(x\) falling in the interval \((x, x + \delta x)\) is given by \(p(x)\delta x\) for \(\delta x \to 0\). The probability that \(x\) lie in an interval \((a, b)\) is then given by:

\[ p(x \in (a, b)) = \int ^{b}_{a}p(x) \, dx \]

The \(p(x)\) must satisfy the two conditions:

\[ \begin{gather} p(x) \geq 0 \\ \int ^{\infty}_{\infty}p(x) \, dx = 1 \end{gather} \]

The probability that \(x\) lies in the interval \((-\infty, z)\) is given by cumulative distribution function:

\[ P(z) = \int _{-\infty}^{z}p(x) \, dx \]

which satisfies \(P'(x) = p(x)\). If we have several continuous variables \(x_{1}, \dots, x_{D}\), denoted collectively b the vector \(\mathrm{x}\), then we can define a joint probability density \(p(\mathrm{x}) = p(x_{1}, \dots, x_{D})\). This multi-variate probability density must satisfy:

\[ \begin{gather} p(\mathrm{x}) \geq 0 \\ \int p(\mathrm{x}) \, d\mathrm{x} = 1 \end{gather} \]

More generally, we can also consider joint probability distribution over a combination of discrete and continuous variables.

2.2.1 Example Distributions

Distributions that cannot be normalised are called improper. We can have a uniform distribution that is constant over a finite region \((c, d)\).

\[ p(x) = 1 / (d - c), \quad x \in (c, d) \]

Another simple form of density is the exponential distribution:

\[ p(x | \lambda) = \lambda \exp(-\lambda x), \quad x \geq 0 \]

Exercise 2.5

A variant of the exponential distribution, known as the Laplace distribution, all the peak moved to a location \(\mu\) and is given by:

\[ p(x | \mu, \gamma) = \frac{1}{2\gamma}\exp\left( - \frac{{|x - \mu|}}{\gamma} \right) \]

Another distribution called Dirac delta function:

\[ p(x | \mu) = \delta(x - \mu) \]

This is defined to be zero everywhere except \(x = \mu\). If we have a finite set of observations of \(x\) given by \(\mathcal{D} = \{ x_{1}, \dots, x_{N}\}\) then we can use the delta function to construct the empirical distribution given by:

\[ p(x | \mathcal{D}) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N}\delta(x - x_{n}) \]

which consists of Dirac delta function centred on each of the data points.

2.2.2 Expectations and covariances

The weighted average of some function \(f(x)\) under a probability distribution is called expectation of \(f(x)\), denoted by \(\mathbb{E}[f]\)

\[ \mathbb{E}[f] = \begin{cases} \sum_{x}p(x)f(x), & \text{discrete distribution} \\ \\ \int p(x)f(x) \, dx & \text{continuous distribution} \end{cases} \]

Exercise 2.7

In either case, if we are given a finite number of \(N\) of points drawn from the probability distribution or probability density, then the expectation can be approximated as:

\[ \mathbb{E}[f] \simeq \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N}f(x_{n}) \]

Sometimes we will be considering expectations of functions of several variables using:

\[ \mathbb{E}_{x}[f(x, y)] \]

denote the average of the function of \(f(x, y)\) with respect to the distribution of \(x\). The \(\mathbb{E}_{x}[x,y]\) will be a function of \(y\).

Exercise 2.11

The conditional expectation with respect to a conditional distribution is:

\[ \mathbb{E}_{x}[f|y] = \begin{cases} \sum_{x}p(x|y)f(x) \\ \\ \int p(x|y) f(x) \, dx \end{cases} \]

which is also a function of \(y\).

Exercise 2.8

The variance of function \(f(x)\) is defined as:

\[ \begin{split} \text{var}[f] &= \mathbb{E}\Big[\big(f(x)-\mathbb{E}[f(x)]\big)^{2}\Big] \\ &= \mathbb{E}[f(x)^{2}] - \mathbb{E}[f(x)]^{2} \\ \end{split} \]

For two random variable \(x\) and \(y\). the covariance measures the extent to which two variables vary together:

\[ \begin{split} \text{cov}[x, y] &= \mathbb{E}_{x,y}[ \{ x - \mathbb{E}[x] \} \{ y - \mathbb{E} [y]\} ] \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{x, y}[xy]-\mathbb{E}[x]\mathbb{E}[y] \\ \end{split} \]

Exercise 2.9

If \(x\) and \(y\) are independent, then their covariance is 0.

For two vector \(x\) and \(y\), their covariance is a matrix:

\[ \begin{split} \text{cov}[\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}] &= \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}}[\{ \mathbf{x} - \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}] \} \{ \mathbf{y}^{\intercal}-\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{y}^{\intercal}] \} ] \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}} [\mathbf{x}\mathbf{y}^{\intercal}] - \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}]\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{y}^{\intercal}]\\ \end{split} \]

Exercise 2.10

If we consider the covariance of the components of the \(x\) then:

\[ \text{cov}[\mathbf{x}] \equiv \text{cov}[\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}] \]

Exercise 2.3

Use the law of total probability, we can get that

\[ p_{\mathbf{y}}(\mathbf{y}) = \int^{\infty}_{\infty}p(\mathbf{y|u})p_{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{u}) \, d\mathbf{u} \]

Since \(\mathbf{y }= \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}\), we have \(\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{u}\), and because \(\mathbf{v}\) and \(\mathbf{u}\) are two independent random variable. So the conditional probability \(p(\mathbf{y | u})\) is the PDF \(p_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{u})\)

\[ p(\mathbf{y | u}) = p_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{v})= p_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{u}) \]

Substitute this in, we get:

\[ p(y) = \int^{\infty}_{\infty}p_{\mathbf{u}}(\mathbf{u}) p_{\mathbf{v}}(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{u}) \, d\mathbf{u} \]

Exercise 2.5

Exponential distribution:

\[ p(x | \lambda) = \lambda \exp(-\lambda x), \quad x \geq 0 \]

Assume \(u = \lambda x\), where \(u \geq 0\) then \(dx = \frac{{du}}{\lambda}\)

\[ \int^{\infty}_{0} \lambda \exp(-\lambda x) \, dx =\int^{\infty}_{0} \exp(u) du=[-e^{-u}]^{\infty}_{0} = (0-(-1)) = 1 \]

Laplace distribution:

\[ p(x | \mu, \gamma) = \frac{1}{2\gamma}\exp\left( - \frac{{|x - \mu|}}{\gamma} \right) \]

$$ \[\begin{split} \int^{\infty}_{\infty}\frac{1}{2\gamma}\exp\left( - \frac{{|x - \mu|}}{\gamma} \right) \, dx = \int^{\infty}_{u}\frac{1}{2\gamma}\exp\left( - \frac{{x - \mu}}{\gamma} \right) \, dx \\ + \int^{u}_{-\infty}\frac{1}{2\gamma}\exp\left( - \frac{{\mu -x }}{\gamma} \right) \, dx \end{split}\]

$$

Integrate each part separately: For \(x \geq u\), assume \(v = \frac{x-u}{\lambda}\), that \(v \geq 0\) and \(dv = \frac{1}{\lambda}dx\)

\[ \begin{gather} \int^{\infty}_{u}\frac{1}{2\gamma}\exp\left( - \frac{{x - \mu}}{\gamma} \right) \, dx \\ = \int^{\infty}_{0} \frac{1}{2}\exp(-v) \, dv \\ = \frac{1}{2}\int^{\infty}_{0}\exp(-v) \, dv \\ = \frac{1}{2}[-e^{-v}]^{\infty}_{0} \\ = \frac{1}{2}(0 - (-1)) = \frac{1}{2} \end{gather} \]

For \(x < u\) assume \(v = \frac{x-u}{\lambda}\), that \(v \leq 0\) and \(dv = \frac{1}{\lambda}dx\)

\[ \begin{gather} \int^{u}_{-\infty}\frac{1}{2\gamma}\exp\left( - \frac{{\mu - x}}{\gamma} \right) \, dx \\ = \int^{0}_{-\infty} \frac{1}{2}\exp(v) \, dv \\ = \frac{1}{2}\int^{0}_{-\infty}\exp(v) \, dv \\ = \frac{1}{2}[e^{v}]^{0}_{-\infty} \\ = \frac{1}{2}(1-0) = \frac{1}{2} \end{gather} \]

So the total sum is 1

Dirac delta function:

\[ p(x | \mu) = \delta(x - \mu) \]

Empirical density function:

\[ p(x | \mathcal{D}) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N}\delta(x - x_{n}) \]

The Dirac Delta function has the following properties:

  1. Sifting Property: \[ \int^{\infty}_{-\infty}f(x)\delta(x-\mu) \, dx = f(\mu) \]
  2. Normalisation \[ \int^{\infty}_{-\infty} \delta(x) \, dx = 1 \]

Check the normalisation of the empirical density function:

\[ \begin{split} \int p(x|\mathcal{D}) \, dx &= \int \left(\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N}\delta(x - x_{n})\right) \, dx \\ &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N}\int \delta(x-x_{n}) \, dx \\ &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N}1 \\ &= 1 \\ \end{split} \]

Exercise 2.40

Prior Probability:

  • \(p(H_{\text{convex}}) = 0.1\)
  • \(p(H_{\text{concave}}) = 1-0.1=0.9\)

Observation:

  • \(N(H) = 8\)
  • \(N(T) = 2\)

Using Bayes’s theorem updated posterior probability

\[ P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)} \]

  1. Calculate likelihood: \[ \begin{gather} p(\text{data}|H_{\text{convex}}) = \binom{10}{8}(0.1)^{8}(0.9)^{2} \\ p(\text{data}|H_{\text{concave}}) = \binom{10}{8}(0.1)^{2}(0.9)^{8} \end{gather} \]
  2. Marginal Likelihood: \[ p(\text{data}) = p(\text{data}|H_{\text{convex}})p(H_{\text{convex}}) + p(\text{data}|H_{\text{concave}})p(H_{\text{concave}}) \]
  3. Calculate the posterior probability: \[ \begin{gather} p(H_{\text{convex}}|\text{data})= \frac{p(\text{data}|H_{\text{convex}})p(H_{\text{convex}})}{p(\text{data})} \approx 0.003 \\ p(H_{\text{concave}}|\text{data})= \frac{p(\text{data}|H_{\text{concave}})p(H_{\text{concave}})}{p(\text{data})} \approx 0.997 \end{gather} \]

So the probability of next flip landing hands is:

\[ p(H) = p(H_{\text{convex}}|\text{data})p(H_{\text{convex}}) + p(H_{\text{concave}}|\text{data})p(H_{\text{concave}}) \approx 0.900 \]
